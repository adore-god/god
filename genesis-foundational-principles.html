Genesis Foundational Principles

As you begin, allow every previous interpretation of the Bible to fall away, for what you will learn here restores Scripture as a revelation of your own consciousness. Its contrasting pairs — like Cain and Abel, and Hagar and Sarah — reveal recurring patterns: Abel, like the Tree of Life, represents the creative, enriched state of being itself being taught; Cain, like the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil, represents everyday consciousness limited by the world. These patterns show the divide between the ordinary mind and the expanded state the Law of Assumption prompts you to embody.

In Genesis 1:1 and Exodus 3:14, God is named 'Judges' and 'Rulers' [of] 'I AM that I AM', followed in Luke 17:21 KJV with

"The kingdom of God is within you" — .

If the kingdom of God is within you, and a kingdom has kings, rulers, lords, judges and laws, then the people and events in the Bible are not external but internal. They are personified movements in consciousness, not records of history.

Genesis lays the foundation of these principles, showing how creation begins within — as imagination, as thought, as desire — before it takes form outwardly.

The Bible is a psychological drama taking place within the reader’s own mind, where every character symbolises a state of self-perception. These figures reflect inner conversations — beliefs, doubts, and desires — that chart the journey of self-awareness. They show the mind learning to apply the law of assumption, gathering its scattered aspects into unity through love. The narrative of the Bible is the psychological return to Eden - that is pleasure and delight

From the opening of Genesis, the Bible defines itself though it's personalised books of identities and mental postures, and through symbols, portraying the head as a house to reveal the workings of the mind. These are the key Bible verses behind Neville Goddard’s core teachings. It is crucial to grasp these concepts at the outset. The entire narrative connects back to them, unveiling a profound mystery. Here are the premises to help the reader begin interpreting Scripture through it's intended narrative:

THE DEFINITION OF GOD: Genesis 1:1

“In the beginning God created…”

The Hebrew word rendered 'God' is Elohim, a plural term meaning judges, rulers, and powers. It symbolises the manifold aspects of your mind and imagination at work. Ezekiel’s “wheels within wheels,” full of eyes, symbolise the self-perceiving and ever-revolving movements of self-perception. The twelve sons, twelve tribes, and twelve disciples are personified examples of these powers convening to support the assumption.

IMAGINATION AS THE SEED OF LIFE: Genesis 1:11

The seed of every experience is planted in imagination. everything reproduces after its kind because the seed is in itself—a direct metaphor for how imagination creates reality. It's the first mention of the two trees in the Garden of Eden.

Read more on this here.

MAN AS INDIVIDUAL PERCEPTION OF SELF: Genesis 1:26

“God said, Let Us make man in Our image.”

Imagination is the union of self-perception and imaginative aspects of mind. The "judges", "rulers" and "powers" of your mind named God, convene to create your perception of self. "Man" refers to your self-concept — who you are aware of being — not a physical form. Any male or female character in the Bible is a psychological concept of your mind.

Read more on this here.

WOMAN AS THE RESPONSE TO SELF-PERCEPTION: Genesis 2:23

"Woman," called forth by man, symbolises your externalised world, every condition and event shaped by what you have accepted as true within. The reflective, remembering mind drawn from the inherent sense of self — the 'I AM that I AM'. Acceptance and mastery gives birth to new states and circumstances.

LOVE: Genesis 2:24

Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and shall cleave to his wife, and they shall be one flesh

One of the most important verses. Here begins the symbolism of love, joining, and marriage within the mind, upon which the rest of the Bible narrative rests.

Sin is first defined here as the difference between imagining delightfully and negatively.

“I AM THAT WHICH I AM”

God is your “I AM” — the awareness of being — seated and conceived in imagination.

Ask, Believe, Receive

"And whatever you ask for in prayer, if you have faith, you will receive it.” — Matthew 21:22

Ask, believe, receive describes an inner process, not a request to an external God. To ask is to form a desire and turn away from old assumptions. To believe is to accept that desire as already fulfilled and remain loyal to that state. To receive is the inevitable result: the inner assumption expresses itself outwardly.
